A glimpse into the world of Voice Leading

Last updated on Jun 18, 2021 4 min read

This post is not finished.

I’ll continue updating it.


I want to describe the surprisingly interesting musical experiment which first piqued my interest into how, from certain simple musical problems, unexpected patterns and relationships can emerge and be, partially, explained through mathematics. However, to make sure that non-musicians can get something out of this post, I’ll begin describing such musical experiment from a mathematical perspective.

The experiment is simple:

  1. Pick any circle.
  2. Choose a chord finite amount of points contained in that circle.
  3. Transpose Rotate each of those notes points, along the chosen circle, by the same amount and in equal direction (clockwise or anticlowise).
  4. We’ll discuss this step later in the post, but the main idea of the following graphs is that every point which has been colored corresponds to a chord finite set of points in the circle, which satisfies a certain symmetry property that I’ll explain at length later in this post.

Visual examples

From a musical perspective, we’ll be dealing with chords (collections of more than two notes) and their transpositions (translating them by some distance), but, from a mathematical perspective, we only need to follow the already mentioned steps 1, 2 and 3.

We’ll work with the unitary circle, the circle with radius 1 and whose center is the origin of the Cartesian Plane. Each point in the unitary circle can be thought of as a possible musical note, if we conveniently match them.

In that sense, let’s work with the following pairing:

Let’s start with a simple case: The major chord

Consider the C major chord, {C, E, G}, which we can represent in the Cartesian Plane as the following set of points:

C : \(\left(\cos(0^{°}),\sin(0^{°})\right)\)

E : \(\left(\cos(120^{°}),\sin(120^{°})\right)\)

G : \(\left(\cos(210^{°}),\sin(210^{°})\right)\)

Now, let’s rotate each of those three points by \(60^{°}\) with respect to the origin, as we can see in the following animation, where the initial points/notes shown correspond to C, E and G, in that order:

From a musical perspective, we’ve transposed the C major chord by 3 semitones and landed on Eb major.

The set of trajectories that the notes from C major take along the unitary circle to get to the notes in Eb major is called the voice leading between C major and Eb major.

Let me clarify that the voice leadings which we’ll be initially dealing with in this post are precisely those which satisfy that no pair of distinct notes from the initial chord end up at the same note in the final chord. In that sense, when we tranpose/rotate any chord with some number \(n\) of notes, the number of possible voice leadings between such chord and its transposition is precisely \(n! = n\cdot(n-1)\cdot(\dots)\cdot 2 \cdot 1 \enspace\).


Going back to our concrete case, let’s see some voice leading examples for the transposition from C major to Eb major:

In this post, we’ll focus on two properties that we’d like a voice leading (which we’ll denote as VL) between a chord and some of its transpositions to have:

  1. The sum of the length of the trajectories which define VL, also known as the voice leading size, is the smallest one out of all the possible voice leadings between the initial chord and its transposition.
  2. The trajectories which define VL share the same direction, that is, they are all clockwise or all anticlockwise.
